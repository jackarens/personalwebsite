=== Birth ===
:'''Born:''' About 1314.
:A precise birth date for Philippa of June 24 either in 1310 or 1311 or 1314 can often be found.<ref>[http://www.oxforddnb.com/view/article/22110 ‘Philippa (1310x15?–1369)’], Oxford Dictionary of National Biography<br>"Her date of birth is not entirely certain, owing to the imprecision of the records as to the order in which she and her sisters were born. Philippa may have been born on 24 June 1310, but it is also possible that she was not born until 1315. "</ref>This comes from a report by Walter de Stapledon, Bishop of Exeter who was sent to Hainaut in 1319 inspect the daughter of William of Hainault as part of the marriage negotiations. He reported ''“the damsel will be of the age of nine years on St. John's day (June 24) next to come.”'' <ref>Hingeston-Randolph. ''Register of Walter de Stapeldon'' (1912): 169. </ref> This is often to be taken to mean that Philippa was born on 24 June since she is the one who was betrothed to Prince Edward in 1326 and married to him in 1328. However, it has also been argued that this report was about Philippa’s older sister Margaret. <ref>Mortimer. ''The Perfect King'' (2008):34.</ref> This certainly seems to be the most likely since Edward II was requesting a papal dispensation for the marriage of his son Edward to Marguerite de Hainaut in 1318 and in 1320.
:Froissart stated that at the time of her marriage she was ''"la joine roine sus le point de quatorze ans."'' <ref>Froissart. ''Chroniques''. (1869):287.</ref> A statement which some have interpreted as meaning she was 14 years old and others that she was almost but not quite 14 years old. Cawley interprets this to mean she was born late January or early February 1314. It must be noted though that Froissart has Edward III's age wrong by one year, which brings into question the accuracy of this date. <ref name="MedLands1"/>
:Richardson simply states that she was likely born between 1313 and 1315. <ref name="Richardson RA"/>
:Wikipedia currently shows a precise birth date of 24 June 1314 which is mixing the statements of Bishop Stapleton and Froissart. <ref name="wiki">[[Wikipedia: Philippa of Hainault]]</ref> The source given for this is Williamson's ''Debrett's Kings and Queens of Britain''. <ref>Williamson's ''Debrett's Kings and Queens of Britain''. (1986):81.</ref>

=== Death and Burial ===:'''Died:''' 15 August 1369 at Windsor Castle in Windsor, Berkshire, England. <ref name="MedLands1"/> <ref name="Richardson RA"/>
:'''Buried:''' 29 January 1370 at [https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Westminster_Abbey Westminster Abbey] in London, England.
:From: Royal Tombs of Medieval England - "Work on the queen's tomb appears to have begun as early as 1362, when instructions were given for six cartloads of alabaster to be brought from the Tutbury quarry to Westminster. On 20 January 1367 'Hawkin Liege' received payment for work on the queen's tomb. Philippa died at Windsor on 10 August (sic) 1369, but it was not until 10 January the following year that Edward instructed the removal of 'dung and other filth' from the London streets in preparation for her funeral. Hearses were commissioned along the processional route at Windsor, Kingston, St. Mary Overy, St. Paul's and Westminster. The queen was buried in the Confessor's Chapel on 29 January 1370. Her tomb, however, was not completed for another six years. On 31 May 1376 the 'stone mason' John Orchard was paid for 'diverse images in the likeness of angels' on her tomb. The tomb must have been largely completed by 28 June 1376 when Orchard received payment for various other works for the tomb-chest and regarding the tomb railings. Philppa's tomb stands in the south-eastern bay of the Confessor's Chapel in Westminster with that of another queen, Eleanor of Castile, in the corresponding bay to the north. Philippa's effigy shows her holding a scepter in the right hand and grasping her mantle cord with the left. The effigy was executed in white marble with the tomb-chest in black marble with white marble cladding. The queen apparently designed her own tomb based on French designs." <ref>Duffy. ''Royal Tombs''. (2003):133-137.</ref>

===Art History===:There is a full-length wooden effigy of Philippa in Queen's College, Oxford. It's not a copy of her tomb effigy, and depicts a younger woman whose features correspond with the Stapledon description. The original college seal also has a full-length figure of the younger queen.
