== Biography ==
John de Yle, was known by many names:Iain, Lord of the Isles, "good John of Islay", owing to his kindness to the Church. He was the son of [[Macdonald-57|Angus Og Macdonald, Lord of the Isles]] and [[O'Cahan-3|Agnes O'Cahan]]
He first married Amy MacRuari, daughter of Roderick, son of Allan MacRuari, his third cousin, for which union they are said to have obtained a papal dispensation,.<ref>J R N Macphail (ed), '''Highland Papers''', 1914, Edinburgh, University Press by T. and A. Conatable for the Scoittish History Society. http://archive.org/stream/highlandpapers01macp#page/73/mode/2up</ref> and had:
1. [[Macdonald-244|John]], whose son Angus is mentioned as one of the hostages given to King David in pledge of the fidelity of the Lord of the Isles. John predeceased his father, and his son Angus does not appear to have left issue.

2. [[MacDonald-198|Reginald or Ranald]], ancestor of the Clanranald.
3. [[Macdonald-213|Godfrey]], founder of the Clan Godfrey (Siol Ghorraidh).
John of Isla is said to have divorced Amy Macruari, his first wife, in favour of the Princess Margaret of Scotland, daughter of Robert II., whom he married as his second wife. By the Princess Margaret he had:

4. [[Macdonald-273|Donald]], his successor.
5. [[MacDonald-177|John Mor Tanistear]], founder of the family of Dunnyveg.

6. [[MacDonald-184|Angus]], who left no issue.
7. [[MacDonald-90|Alexander]], known as Alastair Carrach, founder of the family of Keppoch.
8. [[Macdonald-257|Hugh]], who got a Charter of the Thanage of Glentilt, and whose descendants, according to Skene, became Mclntoshes.
John had also a natural son, Donald, who is mentioned as one of the hostages placed in the King's hands as pledge for his fidelity.
He [John] had a daughter [[MacDonald-173|Mary]], who married Lachlan Lubanach Maclean of Duart, and another daughter [[MacDonald-197|Margaret]], who married [[Macleod-362|Roderick MacLeod]] and perhaps another daughter, Elizabeth, who married [[Mackay-1314|Angus Dubh Mackay]] of Strathnaver.
John's family by the first wife having been cut of from the succession to the lordship of the Isles, John was succeeded by [[Macdonald-273|Donald of Harlaw]].<ref>Macdonald, Rev. A. and Macdonald Rev. A. (1904). '''The Clan Donald: Volume 3'''. Inverness, Scotland. Northern Counties Publishing Company Ltd. http://archive.org/stream/clandonald03macduoft#page/180/mode/2up</ref>
- Sep 12 1335 John formed an alliance with Edward Balliol, where he officially came into control of the lands inherited from his father.

- Sep 19 1356 John was captured at the Battle of Poitiers.
- Dec 16 1357 he was released from captivity in England and granted safe passage to his island home in Scotland.

- soon after his return he likely married Margaret Stewart.

- 1360 he is named Constable of Edinburgh Castle

-1364 he acted as Senescall or High Stewart for the King's household

=== Death ===
The following reference to John is translated from the Macvurich MS. The Macvurichs were a family of the bards and genealogists for Clanranald.
''“This John lived long, and made donations to Icolumkill; he covered the chapel of Eorsay-Elan, the chapel of Finlagam, and the chapel of the Isle of Tsuibhne, and gave the proper furniture for the service of God, upholding the clergy and monks; he built or repaired the church of the Holy Cross immediately before his death. He died at his own castle of Ardtorinish, many priests and monks took the sacrament at his funeral, and they embalmed the body of this dear man, and brought it to Icolumkill; the abbot, monks, and vicar, came as they ought to meet the King of Fiongal, and out of great respect to his memory mourned eight days and eight nights over it, and laid it in the same grave with his father in the church of Oran.”''
John MacDonald was actually murdered by James Campbell. Bain<ref>[[#S-1]] Robert Bain; page 84</ref> provides greater insight into his death. A James Campbell, sent to arrest John MacDonald, during the Regency of the Albany Stewarts, murdered him; "ordered to take John de Islay prisoner, he had exceeded his commission by slaying him". Restitution for this act is noted in the Records of the Parliament of Scotland for the Parliament of James I, at Inverness, in 1428, where, along with imprisoning a number of MacDonald, [[Macdonald-1568|Alexander MacDonald]] and his mother [[Leslie-169|Mary Leslie]], he executed James Campbell.
